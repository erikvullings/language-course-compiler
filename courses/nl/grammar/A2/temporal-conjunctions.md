# Temporal conjunctions

**CEFR level:** A2  
**Introduced in lesson:** 31  
**Depends on:** [Subordinate clauses](subordinate-clauses.md) | [Simple past (weak verbs)](simple-past.md)

---

## Explanation

Temporal conjunctions introduce subordinate clauses that say **when** something happens, happened, or will happen. All of them send the verb to the end of their clause (the standard subordinate clause rule). The key challenge is choosing the right conjunction — Dutch makes distinctions that English collapses into *when*, *after*, and *before*.

---

## The main temporal conjunctions

| Dutch | English | Use |
|-------|---------|-----|
| **toen** | when | single completed event in the past |
| **als** | when / if | repeated events or future |
| **wanneer** | when | future or general time; also indirect questions |
| **terwijl** | while | simultaneous actions |
| **nadat** | after | one action completed before another |
| **voordat** | before | one action happens before another |
| **totdat** | until | continuing until a point |
| **zodra** | as soon as | immediately when |
| **sinds** / **sindsdien** | since | from a past point onwards |
| **zolang** | as long as / for as long as | duration |

---

## *toen* vs *als* vs *wanneer*

This trio is the most commonly confused:

| | *toen* | *als* | *wanneer* |
|--|--------|-------|----------|
| Past single event | ✓ | ✗ | ✗ |
| Habitual / repeated | ✗ | ✓ | ✓ (more formal) |
| Future | ✗ | ✓ | ✓ |
| Indirect question | ✗ | ✗ | ✓ |

**toen** — a specific, once-off moment in the past:
> *Ik was blij **toen** ik het hoorde.* — I was happy when I heard it.  
> *Toen hij binnenkwam, stond iedereen op.* — When he came in, everyone stood up.

**als** — habitual past or future:
> *Als het regent, blijf ik binnen.* — When(ever) it rains, I stay inside.  
> *Ik bel je **als** ik klaar ben.* — I'll call you when I'm done.

**wanneer** — formal equivalent of *als* for future; used in indirect questions:
> *Ik weet niet **wanneer** hij komt.* — I don't know when he's coming.

---

## *terwijl* — simultaneous actions

Both actions are ongoing at the same time. The tense in both clauses is usually the same:

| Dutch | English |
|-------|---------|
| Ze kookt **terwijl** hij de tafel dekt. | She cooks while he sets the table. |
| **Terwijl** ik sliep, belde jij. | While I was sleeping, you called. |
| Hij luisterde naar muziek **terwijl** hij werkte. | He listened to music while he worked. |

---

## *nadat* — after (sequence)

The *nadat*-clause describes an action that is **completed first**; the main clause action follows. Tense interaction: if main clause is past, *nadat*-clause is typically perfect or past perfect:

| Dutch | English |
|-------|---------|
| **Nadat** ze gegeten had, ging ze slapen. | After she had eaten, she went to sleep. |
| **Nadat** de vergadering afgelopen was, gingen we naar huis. | After the meeting had ended, we went home. |
| Ze belde hem **nadat** ze was thuisgekomen. | She called him after she had arrived home. |

---

## *voordat* — before

The *voordat*-clause describes an action that has **not yet happened** when the main clause action takes place:

| Dutch | English |
|-------|---------|
| Ik bel je **voordat** ik vertrek. | I'll call you before I leave. |
| Ze at een boterham **voordat** ze naar school ging. | She ate a sandwich before she went to school. |
| Doe het licht uit **voordat** je de kamer verlaat. | Turn off the light before you leave the room. |

---

## *totdat* — until

| Dutch | English |
|-------|---------|
| Ze wachtte **totdat** de trein aankwam. | She waited until the train arrived. |
| Hij werkte **totdat** het donker was. | He worked until it was dark. |
| Ik blijf hier **totdat** je terugkomt. | I'll stay here until you come back. |

---

## *zodra* — as soon as

| Dutch | English |
|-------|---------|
| **Zodra** ik klaar ben, kom ik. | As soon as I'm done, I'll come. |
| Bel me **zodra** je aankomt. | Call me as soon as you arrive. |

---

## Inversion when the subordinate clause comes first

Like all subordinate clauses, when a temporal clause opens the sentence, inversion applies in the main clause:

> ***Nadat** ze gegeten hadden, **gingen** ze naar huis.*  
> ***Zodra** het stopt met regenen, **gaan** we.**

---

## Examples

| Dutch | English |
|-------|---------|
| **Toen** ik klein was, woonden we in Den Haag. | When I was little, we lived in The Hague. |
| **Als** je tijd hebt, help me dan even. | When you have time, give me a hand. |
| **Terwijl** zij las, keek hij televisie. | While she read, he watched television. |
| **Nadat** hij zijn huiswerk had gemaakt, mocht hij spelen. | After he had done his homework, he was allowed to play. |
| We eten **voordat** de film begint. | We eat before the film starts. |
| Ze bleef oefenen **totdat** ze het kon. | She kept practising until she could do it. |

---

## Common mistakes

**1. Using *als* for a single past event**

*Als ik klein was, woonden we in Amsterdam.* ✗ → ***Toen** ik klein was*, …* ✓

**2. Keeping the verb in position 2 in the temporal clause**

*Nadat ze **heeft** gegeten, ging ze slapen.* ← grammatically *heeft* should be at the end: *Nadat ze gegeten **heeft**, ging ze slapen.* ✓ (or with past perfect: *gegeten had*)

**3. Confusing *voordat* and *nadat***

*Ik belde haar **nadat** ik vertrok.* — I called her after I left (she got the call after departure).  
*Ik belde haar **voordat** ik vertrok.* — I called her before I left. ✓ — check which happened first.

---

## Quick reference

```
toen      →  single past event     (When I was young …)
als       →  habitual or future    (When it rains …)
wanneer   →  future / indirect q.  (I don't know when …)
terwijl   →  simultaneous          (while)
nadat     →  completed first       (after)
voordat   →  not yet done          (before)
totdat    →  continuous limit      (until)
zodra     →  immediate trigger     (as soon as)

All → verb to end; fronted clause → inversion in main clause.
```
