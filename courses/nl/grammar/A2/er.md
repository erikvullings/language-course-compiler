# er

**CEFR level:** A2  
**Introduced in lesson:** 23  
**Depends on:** [Main clause word order](../A1/word-order.md) | [Prepositions of place](../A1/prepositions-of-place.md)

---

## Explanation

**Er** is one of the most versatile and frequently used words in Dutch. It has four distinct functions, all sharing the same form. Learning to recognise which function is active is the key skill.

---

## 1. Existential *er* — there is / there are

Used with *zijn* to state the existence of something. Equivalent to English *there is / there are*.

```
Er  +  is/zijn  +  (article +) noun
Er     is          een probleem.
There  is          a problem.
```

| Dutch | English |
|-------|---------|
| **Er is** een brief voor jou. | There is a letter for you. |
| **Er zijn** veel mensen op straat. | There are many people in the street. |
| **Er was** geen melk meer. | There was no milk left. |
| **Er waren** tien studenten in de klas. | There were ten students in the class. |

> *Er* always occupies position 1 (or position 3 when something else is fronted): *Vandaag **is er** een vergadering.* Note that *er* shifts but the verb stays in position 2.

---

## 2. Locative *er* — there (referring to a place already mentioned)

Replaces a previously mentioned location, equivalent to English *there*:

| Dutch | English |
|-------|---------|
| Ben jij in Amsterdam geweest? — Ja, ik ben **er** vaak. | Have you been to Amsterdam? — Yes, I am there often. |
| Ze werkt bij een bank. **Er** werken veel mensen. | She works at a bank. Many people work there. |

---

## 3. Quantitative *er* — with numbers and quantities

When a quantity is mentioned without specifying the noun, *er* acts as a placeholder:

```
Er  +  verb  +  subject  +  number/quantity
Er     zijn    drie       boeken op tafel.
→ without noun: Hoeveel boeken zijn er? — Er zijn er drie.
```

| Dutch | English |
|-------|---------|
| Hoeveel studenten zijn **er**? | How many students are there? |
| **Er** zijn **er** vijf. | There are five (of them). |
| Ik heb **er** twee gekocht. | I bought two (of them). |
| Heeft hij vrienden? — Hij heeft **er** veel. | Does he have friends? — He has many. |

> Note the double *er* in *Er zijn er vijf*: the first is existential, the second is quantitative (standing in for the noun).

---

## 4. Pronominal *er* — er + preposition (for things)

When a preposition refers back to a non-human noun or idea, Dutch does **not** use *preposition + het*. Instead it forms **er + preposition** (written as one word or with *er* separated):

| Without er (wrong) | With er (correct) | English |
|-------------------|------------------|---------|
| *Ik denk over het.* ✗ | Ik denk **erover**. | I am thinking about it. |
| *Ze wacht op het.* ✗ | Ze wacht **erop**. | She is waiting for it. |
| *Hij houdt van het.* ✗ | Hij houdt **ervan**. | He loves it. |

Common *er* + preposition combinations:

| Combination | Pronunciation | English |
|------------|--------------|---------|
| **erover** | about it | Ik praat erover. |
| **ermee** | with it | Wat doe je ermee? |
| **erop** | on it | De sleutels liggen erop. |
| **erin** | in it | Er zit suiker in — ik proef het erin. |
| **eraan** | to/at it | Ik denk er niet aan. |
| **ervoor** | for it / in front of it | Ze heeft hard ervoor gewerkt. |
| **eruit** | out of it | Haal het eruit. |
| **ernaast** | next to it | De fiets staat ernaast. |

> **er** and the preposition can be split when something comes between them: *Ik denk **er** vaak **over**.* — both forms are correct.

This function of *er* is covered in full detail in [er + preposition](er-preposition.md).

---

## Position of *er* in the clause

*Er* prefers to appear **early** in the clause, typically:
- Position 1 (existential, locative): *Er is een probleem.*
- Right after the finite verb when another element is in position 1: *Vandaag **is er** een vergadering.*
- After the subject in normal SVO order: *Ik heb **er** drie.*

---

## Examples

| Dutch | English | Function |
|-------|---------|----------|
| **Er** staat een fiets voor de deur. | There is a bicycle in front of the door. | existential |
| Hoe lang woon je al **er**? / Woon je al lang **er**? → *Woon je al lang in Utrecht? — Ik woon **er** al vijf jaar.* | I've lived there for five years. | locative |
| Ik heb **er** twee nodig. | I need two (of them). | quantitative |
| Ze is blij **ermee**. | She is happy with it. | pronominal |
| **Er** zijn geen problemen. | There are no problems. | existential |

---

## Common mistakes

**1. Omitting *er* with existential *zijn***

*Is een boek op de tafel.* ✗ → ***Er is** een boek op de tafel.* ✓

**2. Using *het* with a preposition for a thing**

*Ik denk over **het**.* ✗ → *Ik denk **erover**.* ✓

**3. Using *er* for people**

*Er + preposition* is only for things. For people, use *preposition + wie/hem/haar*:  
*Ik praat erover.* (about a thing) vs *Ik praat met **hem**.* (with a person)

---

## Quick reference

```
1. Existential:   er is/zijn + noun   (there is/are)
2. Locative:      er = there (already known place)
3. Quantitative:  er + number, without repeating the noun
4. Pronominal:    er + preposition (for things: erover, ermee, erop …)

Position: early in clause (position 1 or just after finite verb)
```
