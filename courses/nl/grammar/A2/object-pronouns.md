# Object pronouns

**CEFR level:** A2  
**Introduced in lesson:** 22  
**Depends on:** [Subject pronouns](../A1/personal-pronouns.md)

---

## Explanation

When a noun is the **object** of a verb (receiving the action rather than doing it), Dutch uses a different set of pronouns. Like the subject pronouns, most object pronouns have a **stressed** (full) and an **unstressed** (reduced) form.

---

## The object pronouns

| Subject | Object (stressed) | Object (unstressed) | English |
|---------|------------------|---------------------|---------|
| ik | **mij** | **me** | me |
| jij/je | **jou** | **je** | you |
| u | **u** | **u** | you (formal) |
| hij | **hem** | **-** | him |
| zij/ze (sg.) | **haar** | **'r / d'r** | her |
| het | **het** | **'t** | it |
| wij/we | **ons** | **ons** | us |
| jullie | **jullie** | **jullie** | you (pl.) |
| zij/ze (pl.) | **hen** / **hun** | **ze** | them |

---

## Direct and indirect objects

Dutch does not mark the difference between direct (*me*) and indirect (*to me*) objects with different pronoun forms — the same set is used for both:

| Dutch | English |
|-------|---------|
| Hij ziet **mij**. | He sees me. *(direct object)* |
| Hij geeft **mij** een boek. | He gives me a book. *(indirect object)* |
| Ze helpt **hem**. | She helps him. |
| Ik bel **haar** vanavond. | I will call her this evening. |

---

## *hen* vs *hun* (them)

This is one of the trickiest distinctions in Dutch. The prescriptive rule:

- **hen** — direct object (*ik zie hen*)
- **hun** — indirect object (*ik geef hun een boek*)

In practice, many Dutch speakers use **ze** for both in speech. *hen/hun* are more common in formal writing. At A2, use **ze** when in doubt and you will always be understood.

---

## Stressed vs unstressed: when to use which

Use the **stressed** form when:
- the pronoun is emphasised: *Ik meen **jou**, niet hem.*
- the pronoun follows a preposition: *met **mij**, voor **hem**, bij **haar***
- it stands alone as an answer: *Wie belde? — **Mij**.*

Use the **unstressed** form in normal, non-emphatic speech:
- *Ze ziet **me** niet.* (She doesn't see me.)
- *Ik bel **je** morgen.* (I'll call you tomorrow.)

---

## Object pronouns after prepositions

After prepositions, Dutch uses the **stressed** form:

| Dutch | English |
|-------|---------|
| met **mij** | with me |
| voor **hem** | for him |
| bij **haar** | with / at her place |
| zonder **ons** | without us |
| naar **jullie** | to you (all) |

> Exception: for non-human referents, Dutch uses **er + preposition** rather than *het* after a preposition. See [er + preposition](er-preposition.md).

---

## Position of object pronouns

Object pronouns typically follow the finite verb (and any auxiliary):

| Dutch | English |
|-------|---------|
| Ik zie **hem**. | I see him. |
| Ze heeft **mij** geholpen. | She helped me. |
| Kun jij **haar** bellen? | Can you call her? |

Unstressed pronouns tend to cluster immediately after the finite verb, before longer adverbials:

> *Ik bel **je** morgen.* ✓ (not *Ik bel morgen je.*)

---

## Examples

| Dutch | English |
|-------|---------|
| Ken jij **hem**? | Do you know him? |
| Ze geeft **ons** een cadeau. | She gives us a present. |
| Ik zie **ze** morgen. | I'll see them tomorrow. |
| Hij helpt **mij** altijd. | He always helps me. |
| Zonder **jou** is het niet leuk. | Without you it's no fun. |
| We wachten op **jullie**. | We are waiting for you (all). |

---

## Common mistakes

**1. Using subject pronouns as objects**

*Hij ziet zij.* ✗ → *Hij ziet **haar**.* ✓  
*Ik bel hij.* ✗ → *Ik bel **hem**.* ✓

**2. Using *het* after a preposition for a thing**

*Ik praat over **het**.* ✗ → *Ik praat **erover**.* ✓ (see [er + preposition](er-preposition.md))

**3. Confusing *jou* and *je***

*jou* is stressed, *je* is not. After a preposition, always use the stressed form: *voor **jou***, never *voor je* in standard Dutch.

---

## Quick reference

```
ik   → mij / me
jij  → jou / je
u    → u
hij  → hem
zij  → haar / 'r
het  → het / 't
wij  → ons
jullie → jullie
zij (pl.) → hen (direct) / hun (indirect) / ze (informal both)

After prepositions: always stressed form
For things after prepositions: er + preposition (erover, ermee …)
```
