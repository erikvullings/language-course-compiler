# Possessive pronouns

**CEFR level:** A1  
**Introduced in lesson:** 12  
**Depends on:** [Subject pronouns](personal-pronouns.md)

---

## Explanation

Possessive pronouns replace a noun phrase to express ownership. In Dutch they stand **before** the noun (attributive position) and do not change form based on the gender of the noun they precede — with one exception: **ons** vs **onze**.

---

## The possessive pronouns

| Subject | Possessive (stressed) | Possessive (unstressed) | English |
|---------|-----------------------|------------------------|---------|
| ik | **mijn** | **m'n** | my |
| jij/je | **jouw** | **je** | your (informal) |
| u | **uw** | **uw** | your (formal) |
| hij | **zijn** | **z'n** | his |
| zij/ze | **haar** | **d'r / 'r** | her |
| het | — | — | its *(use zijn or omit)* |
| wij/we | **onze** (de-words) / **ons** (het-words) | | our |
| jullie | **jullie** | **jullie** | your (plural) |
| zij/ze (pl.) | **hun** | | their |

> Unstressed forms are common in informal spoken Dutch. In writing and formal speech, use the full forms.

---

## **ons** vs **onze**

This is the one case where the possessive changes based on noun gender:

- **ons** before singular **het**-words: *ons huis, ons kind, ons boek*
- **onze** before **de**-words (singular) and **all plurals**: *onze fiets, onze kinderen, onze stad*

| Dutch | English |
|-------|---------|
| **ons** huis | our house *(het-word)* |
| **onze** tuin | our garden *(de-word)* |
| **onze** kinderen | our children *(plural)* |

---

## No change for other possessives

All other possessives are invariable — they look the same regardless of the noun:

| | de-word | het-word | plural |
|--|---------|----------|--------|
| mijn | mijn fiets | mijn huis | mijn fietsen |
| jouw | jouw fiets | jouw huis | jouw fietsen |
| zijn | zijn fiets | zijn huis | zijn fietsen |
| haar | haar fiets | haar huis | haar fietsen |

---

## *zijn* — verb or pronoun?

*zijn* can mean *to be* (verb) or *his / its* (possessive). Context and position clarify:

- *Hij **is** moe.* — He is tired. (verb)
- *Dit is **zijn** fiets.* — This is his bicycle. (possessive)

---

## Examples

| Dutch | English |
|-------|---------|
| Dit is **mijn** boek. | This is my book. |
| Waar is **jouw** fiets? | Where is your bicycle? |
| **Zijn** huis is groot. | His house is big. |
| **Haar** kinderen zijn lief. | Her children are sweet. |
| **Ons** huis staat in Utrecht. | Our house is in Utrecht. |
| **Onze** hond heet Max. | Our dog is called Max. |
| **Hun** auto is blauw. | Their car is blue. |
| Is dit **uw** jas? | Is this your coat? *(formal)* |

---

## Common mistakes

**1. Using *zijn* for *her***

English *his/her* → Dutch *zijn/haar* — the sounds are similar but opposite:

*Hij is mijn broer. Zijn auto is rood.* — He is my brother. His car is red.  
*Zij is mijn zus. **Haar** auto is rood.* ✓ (not *zijn* for she)

**2. Using *ons* before a *de*-word**

*Ons fiets* ✗ → *Onze fiets* ✓  
*Ons huis* ✓

**3. Using *hun* as subject**

*hun* is the possessive (their); the subject pronoun for *they* is *zij/ze*.

*Hun zijn thuis.* ✗ → ***Zij** zijn thuis.* ✓ / *Hun huis is groot.* ✓

---

## Quick reference

```
mijn / jouw / uw / zijn / haar / ons (het) + onze (de + pl.) / jullie / hun
Unstressed: m'n / je / z'n / d'r
ons + het-word  |  onze + de-word or any plural
```
