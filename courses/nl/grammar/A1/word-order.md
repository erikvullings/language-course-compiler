# Main clause word order

**CEFR level:** A1  
**Introduced in lesson:** 8  
**Depends on:** [Present tense](present-tense.md)

---

## Explanation

Dutch word order is governed by one central rule that distinguishes it from English:

> **The finite verb always occupies the second position (V2) in a main clause.**

"Second position" means second **slot** — not the second word. An adverb or a whole phrase can fill the first slot; the verb immediately follows.

---

## Basic (SVO) order

When the sentence starts with the subject, the word order looks like English:

```
[Subject]  [Verb]  [Rest]
 Ik         werk    thuis.
 He          works   at home.
```

| Dutch | English |
|-------|---------|
| Ik werk thuis. | I work at home. |
| Zij woont in Amsterdam. | She lives in Amsterdam. |
| We eten om zes uur. | We eat at six o'clock. |

---

## Inversion: something other than the subject comes first

When any element other than the subject opens the sentence, the verb still holds position 2 — which means the **subject moves to position 3** (right after the verb). This is called **inversion**.

```
[Adverb / phrase]  [Verb]  [Subject]  [Rest]
 Vandaag            werk    ik         thuis.
 Today               work    I          at home.
```

| Dutch | English |
|-------|---------|
| **Vandaag** werk ik thuis. | Today I work at home. |
| **Om acht uur** vertrekt de trein. | At eight o'clock the train departs. |
| **Morgen** gaan we naar de markt. | Tomorrow we go to the market. |
| **In Utrecht** wonen veel studenten. | In Utrecht many students live. |

> English has a fixed SVO order and does not invert subject and verb in statements. Dutch speakers invert fluently; English speakers must practise this actively.

---

## What goes at the end?

The **rest** of the sentence (objects, adverbials) follows a loose but consistent order. At A1 level, the most important rule is that **infinitives and past participles go to the end**:

- *Ik kan morgen **komen**.* — I can come tomorrow.
- *Wij hebben gisteren **gegeten**.* — We ate yesterday.

This is explored in depth in [Modal verbs](modal-verbs.md) and [Perfect tense](perfect-tense.md).

---

## The verb in a subordinate clause goes last

This rule applies at A2 and beyond. For now, know it exists so surprises make sense later.

---

## Examples

| Dutch | English |
|-------|---------|
| Ik lees een boek. | I read a book. |
| **Nu** lees ik een boek. | Now I read a book. |
| **Elke avond** kijkt hij televisie. | Every evening he watches television. |
| **In de tuin** speelt de hond. | In the garden the dog plays. |
| Morgen kom ik niet. | Tomorrow I am not coming. |

---

## Common mistakes

**1. Keeping SVO after a fronted adverb**

*Vandaag ik werk thuis.* ✗ → *Vandaag **werk** ik thuis.* ✓

**2. Putting the verb at position 3 or later**

The verb is always in position 2, no matter how long the fronted phrase is:

*In het nieuwe huis in Utrecht wonen **we**.* ✓ (verb is still 2nd slot)

**3. Inverting with a pronoun that causes *t*-drop**

When *jij/je* is the subject in an inverted sentence, the verb loses its *-t*:

*Vandaag werk**t** jij thuis?* ✗ → *Vandaag **werk** jij thuis?* ✓

---

## Quick reference

```
Position 1  →  subject  OR  any fronted element (adverb, object, phrase)
Position 2  →  finite verb  (always — the V2 rule)
Position 3  →  subject (if position 1 was not the subject)
...rest...  →  objects, other adverbials
End         →  infinitives, past participles
```
