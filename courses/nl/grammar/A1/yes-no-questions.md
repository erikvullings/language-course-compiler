# Yes/no questions

**CEFR level:** A1  
**Introduced in lesson:** 9  
**Depends on:** [Main clause word order](word-order.md)

---

## Explanation

A yes/no question (*ja/nee-vraag*) is formed by moving the finite verb to **position 1** — directly before the subject. This is the same inversion rule seen in statements with fronted adverbs, but now the verb itself starts the sentence.

```
Statement:   [Subject]  [Verb]  [Rest]
Question:    [Verb]     [Subject]  [Rest]  ?

Jij werkt thuis.   →   Werk jij thuis?
```

---

## Forming yes/no questions

1. Take the statement.
2. Move the finite verb to position 1.
3. The subject follows immediately.
4. Add a question mark.

| Statement | Yes/no question |
|-----------|----------------|
| Jij werkt hier. | **Werk** jij hier? |
| Hij heeft een auto. | **Heeft** hij een auto? |
| Ze zijn moe. | **Zijn** ze moe? |
| Jullie spreken Nederlands. | **Spreken** jullie Nederlands? |
| U woont in Amsterdam. | **Woont** u in Amsterdam? |

---

## The *t*-drop in questions

When the subject is **jij** or **je** (not *u*), the verb loses its *-t* in inversion:

| With *u* (no drop) | With *jij/je* (t-drop) |
|--------------------|------------------------|
| Werkt **u** hier? | Werk **jij** hier? |
| Heeft **u** tijd? | Heb **jij** tijd? |

> The *t*-drop only applies to *jij/je*. With all other subjects the verb form stays the same as in the statement.

---

## Short answers

Dutch yes/no questions are answered with short phrases:

| Answer | Dutch | Informal |
|--------|-------|---------|
| Yes | **Ja.** | **Ja, hoor.** (reassuring yes) |
| No | **Nee.** | **Nee, hoor.** |
| Yes (contradicting a negative) | **Jawel.** | |

*Hoor* after *ja* or *nee* softens the answer — a very Dutch habit worth noticing.

---

## Rising intonation

Dutch yes/no questions are typically spoken with a **rising pitch** at the end, just like English. This is an important cue for the listener.

---

## Examples

| Dutch question | English |
|----------------|---------|
| **Ben** jij student? | Are you a student? |
| **Heb** je een fiets? | Do you have a bicycle? |
| **Is** het koud vandaag? | Is it cold today? |
| **Werkt** hij in Rotterdam? | Does he work in Rotterdam? |
| **Spreken** jullie Frans? | Do you (all) speak French? |
| **Heeft** u een tafel vrij? | Do you have a table free? *(formal)* |

---

## Common mistakes

**1. Adding *do / does* logic from English**

Dutch has no auxiliary verb for simple questions. Never add *doen*:

*Doe jij werken hier?* ✗ → *Werk jij hier?* ✓

**2. Forgetting the *t*-drop with *jij/je***

*Werkt jij hier?* ✗ → *Werk jij hier?* ✓

**3. Keeping the statement order**

*Jij werkt hier?* — this is actually possible in Dutch (rising intonation turns a statement into a question in informal speech), but the standard inversion form is preferred in writing and formal speech.

---

## Quick reference

```
Statement:  Subject  +  Verb  +  rest
Question:   Verb     +  Subject  +  rest  ?

t-drop:  always with jij/je in inversion  (never with u, hij, zij, etc.)
```
