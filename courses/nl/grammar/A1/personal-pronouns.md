# Subject pronouns

**CEFR level:** A1  
**Introduced in lesson:** 1  
**Depends on:** —

---

## Explanation

Dutch has a full set of **subject pronouns** — the words that replace a noun when it is the subject of a sentence. Every pronoun has a *stressed* (full) form and an *unstressed* (reduced) form. The reduced forms are used in normal, relaxed speech; the full forms carry extra emphasis.

| Person | Stressed | Unstressed | English |
|--------|----------|------------|---------|
| 1st sg. | **ik** | **'k** | I |
| 2nd sg. informal | **jij** | **je** | you |
| 2nd sg. formal | **u** | **u** | you (polite) |
| 3rd sg. masc. | **hij** | **-** | he |
| 3rd sg. fem. | **zij** | **ze** | she |
| 3rd sg. neuter | **het** | **'t** | it |
| 1st pl. | **wij** | **we** | we |
| 2nd pl. | **jullie** | **jullie** | you (plural) |
| 3rd pl. | **zij** | **ze** | they |

> **Note:** *zij* / *ze* covers both *she* and *they*. Context always makes the meaning clear.

---

## Formal vs informal: **u** vs **jij/je**

This is the most socially important distinction for a beginner.

- **jij / je** — used with friends, family, children, and peers. Default in casual conversation.
- **u** — used with strangers you want to show respect to, older people, customers, and in formal writing. Overusing *u* with young people can sound oddly stiff.

> *Hoe heet **jij**?* — What is your name? (to a friend)  
> *Hoe heet **u**?* — What is your name? (formally)

## Examples

| Dutch | English |
|-------|---------|
| **Ik** ben Erik. | I am Erik. |
| **Jij** bent student. | You are a student. |
| **Hij** werkt hier. | He works here. |
| **Zij** is mijn zus. | She is my sister. |
| **Het** is koud. | It is cold. |
| **Wij** wonen in Amsterdam. | We live in Amsterdam. |
| **Jullie** spreken Nederlands. | You (all) speak Dutch. |
| **Zij** komen morgen. | They come tomorrow. |

## Common mistakes

**1. Confusing *zij* (she) and *zij* (they)**

Both use *zij* / *ze*. The verb form tells you which is which:

- *Ze **is** moe.* → she is tired (singular verb)
- *Ze **zijn** moe.* → they are tired (plural verb)

**2. Using *het* for people**

*Het* refers to things and neuter nouns, not people. Say *hij* or *zij*, never *het*, for a person.

**3. Forgetting the two forms**

*Jij* is stressed; *je* is unstressed. When *jij* or *je* follows the verb (inversion), the -*t* ending on the verb disappears — but only with *jij/je*, not with *u*:

- *Jij **werkt**.* → normal
- ***Werk** jij hier?* → the *t* drops (inversion with *jij*)
- ***Werkt** u hier?* → the *t* stays (inversion with *u*)

This verb-*t* drop is a feature of *jij/je* only and is covered in detail in the [present-tense](present-tense.md) lesson.
