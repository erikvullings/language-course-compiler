# Future with gaan

**CEFR level:** A1  
**Introduced in lesson:** 17  
**Depends on:** [Present tense](present-tense.md) | [Main clause word order](word-order.md)  
**Signal words:** *morgen, straks, volgende week, vanavond*

---

## Explanation

Dutch has no dedicated future tense. The most common way to express **future plans and intentions** is with the verb **gaan** (to go) + infinitive — directly parallel to English *going to*:

```
[Subject]  [gaan conjugated]  [Rest]  [Infinitive]
 Ik         ga                 morgen   werken.
 I           am going           tomorrow to work.
```

The present tense is also widely used for future events (especially scheduled ones), but *gaan* + infinitive specifically signals a **planned or intended** future action.

---

## Conjugation of **gaan**

| Subject | Form |
|---------|------|
| ik | **ga** |
| jij/je | **gaat** |
| u | **gaat** |
| hij/zij/het | **gaat** |
| wij/we | **gaan** |
| jullie | **gaan** |
| zij/ze (pl.) | **gaan** |

**t-drop with jij/je in inversion:**  
*Ga jij morgen werken?* (not *gaat*)

---

## Word order

*Gaan* occupies position 2 (the finite verb slot). The infinitive goes to the **end** of the clause — same pattern as modal verbs:

| Dutch | English |
|-------|---------|
| Ik ga morgen **werken**. | I am going to work tomorrow. |
| Ze gaat vanavond **koken**. | She is going to cook this evening. |
| We gaan volgende week **reizen**. | We are going to travel next week. |

With a fronted time expression, *gaan* stays in position 2:

> *Morgen ga ik **werken**.* — Tomorrow I am going to work.  
> *Straks gaan we **eten**.* — In a bit we are going to eat.

---

## Signal words for future plans

| Dutch | English |
|-------|---------|
| **morgen** | tomorrow |
| **straks** | in a little while / soon |
| **vanavond** | this evening |
| **volgende week** | next week |
| **volgende maand** | next month |
| **later** | later |
| **binnenkort** | soon / shortly |

These words help signal that *gaan* is expressing a future plan rather than literal movement.

---

## *gaan* for literal movement vs future

*Gaan* also means *to go* literally. Context distinguishes:

| Dutch | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| Ik ga naar de winkel. | I am going to the shop. *(literal)* |
| Ik ga boodschappen doen. | I am going to do the shopping. *(future plan)* |
| Hij gaat morgen **vertrekken**. | He is going to leave tomorrow. *(future intent)* |

---

## Present tense for scheduled future

The simple present is preferred for **fixed schedules**:

> *De trein vertrekt om tien uur.* — The train leaves at ten o'clock.  
> *Morgen **werk** ik thuis.* — Tomorrow I work from home. *(scheduled)*

Use *gaan* when the emphasis is on **intention or plan**:

> *Ik ga morgen thuis **werken**.* — I am going to work from home tomorrow. *(deliberate choice)*

---

## Examples

| Dutch | English |
|-------|---------|
| Ik ga **straks** een boek **lezen**. | I am going to read a book in a bit. |
| **Gaat** ze **morgen** naar Amsterdam? | Is she going to Amsterdam tomorrow? |
| We gaan **volgende week** **verhuizen**. | We are going to move next week. |
| Hij gaat een nieuwe fiets **kopen**. | He is going to buy a new bicycle. |
| **Ga** jij ook mee**doen**? | Are you going to join in too? |
| Ze gaan **vanavond** uit **eten**. | They are going to eat out this evening. |

---

## Common mistakes

**1. Putting the infinitive in position 2**

*Ik ga lezen een boek morgen.* ✗ → *Ik ga morgen een boek **lezen**.* ✓

**2. Forgetting *t*-drop with *jij/je* in questions**

*Gaat jij mee?* ✗ → ***Ga** jij mee?* ✓

**3. Conjugating both verbs**

*Ik ga werkt.* ✗ → *Ik ga **werken**.* ✓ (infinitive never conjugated)
