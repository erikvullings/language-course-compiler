# Indefinite article (een)

**CEFR level:** A1  
**Introduced in lesson:** 5  
**Depends on:** [Definite articles](articles.md)

---

## Explanation

The indefinite article in Dutch is **een** — equivalent to English *a* or *an*. Unlike the definite articles *de* and *het*, **een is the same for all nouns**, regardless of gender.

| | Indefinite | Definite |
|--|------------|---------|
| *de*-word | **een** fiets | **de** fiets |
| *het*-word | **een** huis | **het** huis |

---

## Pronunciation

**een** as an article is always **unstressed** and pronounced like a short schwa: roughly *uh*. It sounds nothing like **één** (one, the numeral), which is stressed and has a long vowel.

- *een boek* — a book *(unstressed, schwa)*
- *één boek* — one book *(stressed, long vowel — written with an accent to mark the stress)*

---

## When to use **een**

Use *een* to introduce a noun for the first time or when referring to any member of a category:

| Dutch | English |
|-------|---------|
| Ik heb **een** fiets. | I have a bicycle. |
| Hij koopt **een** boek. | He is buying a book. |
| Ze zoekt **een** huis. | She is looking for a house. |

Once the noun is established in the conversation, switch to *de* or *het*:

> *Ik heb **een** fiets. **De** fiets is blauw.*  
> I have **a** bicycle. **The** bicycle is blue.

---

## No article in Dutch = English *some* or uncountable

Dutch often drops the article entirely where English uses *some* or for uncountable nouns:

| Dutch | English |
|-------|---------|
| Ik drink koffie. | I drink coffee. |
| Hij eet brood. | He eats bread. |
| We hebben tijd. | We have time. |

---

## een and negation

When negating a noun with an indefinite article or no article at all, use **geen** instead of *niet*. This replaces *een*:

| Positive | Negative |
|---------|---------|
| Ik heb **een** auto. | Ik heb **geen** auto. |
| Hij drinkt koffie. | Hij drinkt **geen** koffie. |

See [Negation](negation.md) for the full rules.

---

## Adjective exception with *een* + *het*-word

When an adjective comes before a singular *het*-word with *een*, the adjective does **not** get the -*e* ending:

| | *de*-word | *het*-word |
|--|-----------|-----------|
| definite | de groot**e** fiets | het groot**e** huis |
| **indefinite** | een groot**e** fiets | een groot huis ← **no -e** |

This is the one exception to the adjective rule and is covered fully in [Attributive adjectives](adjectives.md).

---

## Examples

| Dutch | English |
|-------|---------|
| Dit is **een** goed idee. | This is a good idea. |
| Ze heeft **een** vraag. | She has a question. |
| Ik zie **een** auto. | I see a car. |
| Is dit **een** restaurant? | Is this a restaurant? |
| We zoeken **een** kamer. | We are looking for a room. |

---

## Common mistakes

**1. Using *de* or *het* instead of *een***

*Ik heb de fiets.* means *I have **the** bicycle* (a specific one already mentioned).  
*Ik heb **een** fiets.* means *I have **a** bicycle* (any bicycle).

**2. Confusing the article *een* with the number *één***

The article is never stressed or written with an accent. If you mean *one*, write *één*.

**3. Forgetting *geen* for negation**

*Ik heb niet een auto.* ✗ → *Ik heb **geen** auto.* ✓

---

## Quick reference

```
een  →  a / an  (same for de- and het-words)
geen →  no / not a  (negated form of een, also negates uncountable nouns)
```
